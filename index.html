The Newcastle Poetry Festival 2018 coincides with a major retrospective of the artist Sean Scully, co-hosted by the Hatton and Laing Art Galleries. Scully, who was born in Ireland, grew up in England and who has lived for many years in New York, is a graduate of Newcastle University. He is famous around the world as the master of post-minimalist abstraction and this retrospective is devoted to the work he undertook as an undergraduate here.

The theme of this year’s Newcastle Poetry Festival is Crossings, and we’re focusing on all kinds of marriages and interweavings – between languages and cultures, but also between art forms. Scully’s work is itself characterised by multiple intersections as it explores the tensions between image and pattern, change and stasis, purity and impurity, surface and depth, and takes inspiration from the civic architecture of Newcastle, and in particular the bridges spanning the Tyne.

Now in his seventies, Scully has twice been shortlisted for the Turner Prize and his work is in the collection of every major gallery in the world.

As part of the 2018 Newcastle Poetry Festival, eight poets have been commissioned to respond to eight paintings exhibited in Sean Scully: 1970, a major retrospective co-hosted by the Hatton and Laing Art Galleries between February and May 2018.

The Crossings poetry app allows visitors to both galleries to read and listen to the poets' work while looking at the art in situ. From the Home screen, simply select the gallery you are currently visiting and then choose the poem you would like to read or hear by selecting the painting that inspired it. Use the 'Aa' button when reading a poem to adjust the text size. Information about the artist and the poets involved in the project can be found by selecting the tabs at the top of this screen.

The two galleries are only a short walk from one another and we encourage you to take the opportunity to explore the city. Visitors to Newcastle can tap here to see the route between the Hatton Gallery and and Laing Art Gallery on Google Maps.

Thanks are due to the following: Sean Scully for his blessing on this project and to Faye Fleming for her help with logistics; to the Newcastle Institute for Creative Arts Practice for funding this project as an interdisciplinary “crossing” between visual art and poetry; and to Tyne and Wear Archives and Museums for hosting our poets and enabling the conversation.
